# Chapter Eight: Cleared for Tobago

**[T.A.I. Personnel File #2847 - Tobago Facility Access Log]**  
**Date: August 28, 2043**  
**Time: 08:15 AM - 06:42 PM**  
**Security Level: ELEVATED - External Observation Detected**

---

The ferry from Port of Spain to Scarborough took three hours. Three hours of open water, Caribbean sun, and growing anticipation mixed with exhaustion from the all-nighter.

Dr. Lal slept through most of it. Raj alternated between napping and scrolling through technical documents on his tablet. I stood at the railing, watching Trinidad shrink behind us and Tobago grow ahead, three trucks secured in the ferry's cargo hold below.

"First time to Tobago?" Maya appeared beside me, her own coffee in hand.

"Yeah. Never had much reason to go before."

"It's different from Trinidad. Quieter. More rural. But beautiful in its own way." She sipped her coffee. "The test site is isolated. You'll understand why when you see it. We need the separation."

"For safety?"

"Partially. But also..." she hesitated, choosing words carefully. "There are aspects of PROMETHEUS we haven't told you about yet. Not because we don't trust you, but because they require seeing to believe. Tonight, after we arrive and get settled, there'll be another briefing. The full picture."

"I thought I had the full picture. Metallic hydrogen propulsion."

"That's the foundation. But Dr. Ramcharan didn't spend seven years and all this money just to make rockets slightly more efficient." Maya looked out at the ocean. "She's always thinking three steps ahead. PROMETHEUS is step one. What comes after... that's what tomorrow's test is really about."

Before I could press for more information, Gabi's voice called from behind us. "Maya! Dr. Ramcharan wants you. Something about the Tobago facility reporting unusual activity."

Maya's expression shifted immediately from casual to alert. "What kind of activity?"

"Didn't say. Just get up to the truck deck."

We followed Gabi down to where the trucks were parked. Dr. Ramcharan had set up a makeshift command post in the lead truck's cab, laptop open, phone pressed to her ear. Colonel Thompson was there too, which was surprising—I hadn't realized he was on the ferry.

"When?" Dr. Ramcharan was saying. "And you're certain it was a drone?... No, don't engage. Just monitor and document. We'll be there in two hours." She hung up. "We have a complication."

"What kind?" Maya asked.

"Zay spotted an unidentified drone conducting surveillance of the test site. Small, commercial-grade, but equipped with high-resolution cameras. It made three passes over the facility perimeter before leaving toward the northeast."

The temperature in the truck cab seemed to drop.

"Could be a coincidence," Gabi suggested. "Lots of people use drones. Tourists, photographers—"

"At 7 AM on a Sunday morning, flying systematic search patterns over what's supposed to be an agricultural research station in the middle of nowhere?" Colonel Thompson's skepticism was sharp. "That's not a tourist."

"Do we abort?" Wrench asked. He'd been checking on the cargo and had joined the impromptu meeting.

"No." Dr. Ramcharan was firm. "We've come too far, and we need this test data. But we operate under the assumption we're being watched. Heightened security protocols. No external communications except through encrypted channels. And we move up the test timeline."

"How much?" Dr. Lal had appeared, drawn by the tension.

"Tomorrow morning. First light. We'll work through the night if we have to, but I want that engine burning and the test complete before whoever's watching has time to organize a response."

"That's aggressive," Maya said. "We haven't even integrated the engine yet. Normally that's a two-day process—"

"Then we do it in twelve hours." Dr. Ramcharan closed her laptop. "I knew this day would come eventually. Someone noticing. Someone curious. We're not ready to go public yet, but we're ready enough. We prove PROMETHEUS works tomorrow, document everything, and then we control the narrative before anyone else can."

She looked at each of us. "This is what we prepared for. Calculated risk becomes real risk. Are we ready?"

One by one, we nodded.

"Good. When we dock, we move fast. Straight to the facility, no delays. Colonel Thompson, you coordinate security. I want eyes on every approach route. If that drone comes back, I want to know immediately."

---

The Tobago facility was nothing like the Trinidad operation.

We drove inland from Scarborough, following increasingly rough roads through dense jungle. The kind of roads that didn't appear on most maps, that required four-wheel drive and local knowledge to navigate. After forty-five minutes, we turned onto an unmarked track that wound through breadfruit and coconut trees, the ocean visible occasionally through gaps in the vegetation.

Then we emerged into a clearing.

The official story was "Northeast Agricultural Research Station"—and there were enough legitimate agricultural operations to sell that cover. Test plots for drought-resistant crops. A small greenhouse complex. Equipment sheds that actually held farming equipment.

But past that, hidden by careful landscaping and natural terrain, was the real facility.

The launch site sat in a natural amphitheater formed by hills on three sides, opening to the east toward the Atlantic. The pad itself was smaller than I expected—maybe fifty meters square—but clearly built to serious specifications. Flame trench, water deluge system, fuel storage bunkers set safely back from the pad. A reinforced control bunker with thick windows facing the launch area.

And beyond everything, the ocean. Endless blue stretching to the horizon, with waves breaking on rocky shoreline maybe two hundred meters from the pad.

"Beautiful, yeah?" Zay appeared from one of the equipment buildings. He looked tired but wired, the energy of someone who'd been preparing alone all week. "Been working here two years, still never get tired of the view."

"It's perfect," I said.

"It's strategic," Dr. Ramcharan corrected. "Open ocean to the east for debris safety. Hills provide natural sound baffling. Isolated enough that we can control access. And the wind patterns are favorable most of the year."

We started unloading immediately. The engine components came out first, each case carefully transported to the integration building—a climate-controlled structure where the final assembly would happen. The metallic hydrogen containers required even more care, with Colonel Thompson personally supervising their transfer to a secure storage bunker.

"We have motion sensors on every approach route," Zay briefed us. "Cameras with IR and night vision. Radar system that can track anything larger than a bird within five kilometers. That drone this morning—I've got footage of it. Want to see?"

He pulled up the security video on a tablet. The drone was indeed commercial-grade, probably a DJI or similar. But the flight pattern was too systematic, too professional. This wasn't a hobbyist.

"Whoever's flying this knows what they're looking for," Colonel Thompson observed. "They did a full perimeter survey, took high-res photos of every structure, then left. Professional intelligence gathering."

"Which government?" Maya asked.

"Could be anyone. US, China, Russia, EU—anyone with satellite capability probably knows something is happening here. But satellites can't see inside buildings or understand what we're actually doing. Hence the drone."

"Will they come back?" I asked.

"Definitely," Colonel Thompson said. "Question is when. Which is why we're accelerating the timeline."

---

The afternoon was a blur of activity. The integration building became a hive of controlled chaos as Wrench's team began reassembling the engine with the same meticulous care they'd used to disassemble it. Dr. Lal supervised every connection, every seal, every component.

I helped where I could, but mostly I was learning the Tobago facility's layout. The control bunker was more sophisticated than I expected, with redundant systems for telemetry, safety monitoring, and command. The fuel systems were impressive—cryogenic storage for the oxidizer, specialized containment for the metallic hydrogen, all designed to military-grade specifications.

"We didn't build this in two years," I said to Gabi as we inspected the control systems.

"No. This facility has been operational for five years. Dr. Ramcharan started construction before the Trinidad lab was even finished. She knew we'd need a test site eventually." Gabi pulled up facility schematics. "What you're seeing is phase one. There's a phase two planned—expanded pad, larger support buildings, accommodation for bigger test articles."

"Bigger than a sixty-pound engine?"

"Much bigger." She gave me a significant look. "Tonight's briefing. You'll understand then."

---

As the sun started setting, painting the ocean in oranges and purples, Dr. Ramcharan called a meeting in the control bunker's conference room. The core team only—her, Maya, Dr. Lal, Gabi, Wrench, Colonel Thompson, Zay, Raj, and me. Nine people who knew everything.

"Before tomorrow's test," Dr. Ramcharan began, "you need to understand what we're actually testing."

She pulled up a schematic on the main display. It showed the engine we'd been working on, but it wasn't mounted on a test stand. It was integrated into... something else.

A vehicle.

Small, maybe eight feet long, sleek and aerodynamic. The engine mounted at the rear, fuel tanks integrated into the body, what looked like guidance systems and payload bay in the nose.

"This is ICARUS," Dr. Ramcharan said. "The test vehicle we've been building in parallel with the propulsion system."

"An actual rocket," I breathed. "Not just an engine test."

"Correct. Tomorrow we're not testing static thrust. We're testing a complete flight system." She advanced the schematic, showing the flight profile. "Launch to 100 kilometers altitude, coast phase, controlled descent and ocean recovery. Total flight time approximately twelve minutes. It's a suborbital demonstration, but it proves everything—propulsion, guidance, thermal protection, recovery systems."

"This is what was behind that fourth door in Trinidad," I said, pieces clicking together. "The room with the natural light. You were building the vehicle there."

"Partially. Final integration happens here in Tobago. But yes, most of ICARUS was manufactured in Trinidad and transported here over the past month."

Dr. Lal pulled up more technical details. "The metallic hydrogen provides enough specific impulse that we can reach 100 kilometers with a vehicle this small. With conventional propulsion, you'd need something ten times larger. That's the revolution—access to space becomes portable, affordable, rapid."

"But there's more," Maya said. She looked nervous, which was unusual for her. "Something we discovered during the stabilization research. An unexpected side effect of the electromagnetic containment fields we use to keep the metallic hydrogen stable."

She pulled up new data—graphs showing electromagnetic field measurements, quantum states, things I didn't fully understand.

"The containment field creates a localized distortion in quantum vacuum fluctuations," she explained. "It's subtle, barely measurable. But when you scale up the field strength to what we need for active propulsion, the effect becomes significant."

"Significant how?" I asked.

Dr. Ramcharan took over. "We don't fully understand the mechanism yet. But during high-power operation, the metallic hydrogen engine appears to interact with zero-point energy fields. The thrust we measure is approximately 2-3% higher than can be explained by pure chemical propulsion."

The room was silent.

"That's impossible," I finally said. "That's... you're describing—"

"Something that violates conservation of momentum as currently understood," Dr. Lal finished. "Yes. We've run the tests dozens of times. The effect is real and reproducible. It's small—barely above measurement error—but it's there."

"Reactionless thrust," Gabi said. "Or at least, partially reactionless. Most of the thrust is still conventional propulsion, but there's a component that seems to come from interaction with quantum vacuum."

"This is the real reason for secrecy," Dr. Ramcharan said. "If we announced metallic hydrogen propulsion alone, it would be revolutionary but explainable. This—" she gestured at the data, "—this breaks physics. Or at least breaks our current understanding of physics. If this is real, if we can understand and scale it, we're not just talking about better rockets. We're talking about potentially violating the rocket equation entirely."

"Does it work at higher power levels?" I asked.

"We don't know yet. Tomorrow's test will be the highest power, longest duration burn we've attempted. If the effect scales with power, we might see 5-10% anomalous thrust. That would be unambiguous proof that something extraordinary is happening."

"And if foreign governments learn about this before we understand it..." Colonel Thompson didn't need to finish the sentence.

"Exactly," Dr. Ramcharan confirmed. "Metallic hydrogen is valuable. But if we've accidentally discovered a pathway to modifying inertia or tapping zero-point energy, that's potentially civilization-changing. And definitely something every military in the world would kill to control."

She let that sink in.

"So tomorrow, we test ICARUS. We prove metallic hydrogen propulsion works in real flight conditions. And we gather data on this quantum anomaly. If everything works, we have evidence of two revolutionary breakthroughs. Then we go public on our terms, with proof, before anyone can claim it or suppress it."

"What if the test fails?" Raj asked.

"Then we learn and iterate. But we do it quickly, before whoever sent that drone decides to take more aggressive action."

Zay spoke up from his corner. "I've been running the flight profile simulations all week. ICARUS is ready. The vehicle is solid, the trajectory is clean, the recovery systems are tested. We're as ready as we can be."

"The engine control algorithms are proven," I added. "The active stability management system worked perfectly in Trinidad. Should handle any instabilities that develop during flight."

"Structural components are within spec," Wrench confirmed. "Every weld inspected, every joint verified. The hardware won't fail."

Dr. Ramcharan looked around the room. "Then we proceed. Integration continues tonight. Final checks at midnight. Fueling at 4 AM. Launch window opens at 6:15 AM, right as sun breaks the horizon. We'll have perfect lighting for tracking cameras and clear weather according to forecasts."

"And if the drone comes back?" Colonel Thompson asked.

"Then it gets spectacular footage of something it won't understand. Let them watch. By the time they figure out what they've seen, we'll have already announced it to the world."

---

The next six hours were the most intense yet. We worked through the night under portable lights, integrating the engine into ICARUS's airframe. Every connection had to be perfect. Every system tested and retested.

The vehicle was beautiful up close. Sleek carbon fiber construction, aerodynamic surfaces designed for hypersonic flight, heat-resistant coating for reentry. And at its heart, our sixty-pound engine that broke the rules of propulsion.

Around 2 AM, I took a break and walked out to look at the ocean. The waves were steady, rhythmic. Stars blazed overhead with the clarity you only get away from city lights. The Milky Way stretched across the sky, billions of stars, countless worlds.

Tomorrow, we'd take a step toward reaching them.

"Can't sleep?" Maya had followed me out.

"Too wired. Too much in my head."

"I know the feeling." She stood beside me, looking up. "You know what Dr. Ramcharan told me when she first recruited me back to Trinidad? She said 'Humanity is ready for the stars, but the stars won't wait forever. We either reach them now, or we stay trapped until something—climate, war, disaster—ends our chance.' That's why she's pushing so hard. Why the secrecy, the risk, all of it. She thinks we're running out of time."

"Do you agree with her?"

"I don't know. But I know that if we don't try, we definitely fail. So we might as well try."

Raj joined us, then Gabi, then others. The team gathering at the edge of the world, waiting for dawn and history.

"Whatever happens tomorrow," Gabi said, "we built something real. Solved problems people said were impossible. That matters, regardless of the test outcome."

"The test will work," Dr. Lal declared, arriving with his perpetual coffee mug. "The mathematics is sound. The engineering is solid. The only unknown is the quantum anomaly, and even if that doesn't scale as predicted, we still have revolutionary propulsion."

"No pressure," I said, echoing Dr. Ramcharan's words from days before.

"All the pressure," Dr. Lal grinned. "But you've proven you work well under pressure. That's why you're here."

At 4 AM, we began fueling operations. Colonel Thompson supervised the metallic hydrogen loading—one precious cylinder, 100 grams, worth more than gold and infinitely more valuable. The oxidizer came next, cryogenic and dangerous in its own right.

ICARUS sat on the pad, fueled and ready, gleaming under floodlights. Eight feet of carbon fiber and exotic metals and impossible propellant, about to attempt something that shouldn't work.

Dr. Ramcharan made a final inspection, walking around the vehicle, checking connections, verifying everything one last time. When she was satisfied, she nodded to Zay.

"Move it to launch position. Let's make history."

The sun was just starting to lighten the eastern horizon as ICARUS was positioned on the pad, pointed toward the sky, waiting for fire.

And somewhere out there, maybe, someone was watching.

Let them watch, I thought. Let them see what happens when brilliant people decide the future is worth fighting for.

Dawn was coming.

And with it, everything would change.
